Components of User Experience

There is not only one correct answer to the question of how to manage emotional customer reactions, it will always be dependent on context and character, as well as myriad other factors which make up the complexity of human relationships. To consider some possible appraches, we can talk more broadly about how the project manager might decide on the correct approach to take, given a certain situation.

I believe the first act of the Project Manager should always be to evaluate the source of the emotion, before taking any action. Although some emotions are an acceptable part of work (Lindgren et al, 2014), often they can manifest in a way which is unacceptable and not productive to the project delivery. The Project Manager must first use their emotional intelligence to empathise with the customer, understand the source of the emotion, and then decide if it is something which can or should be tackled. The requirement for the Project Manager to do this successfully is one of the reasons that emotional intelligence has become widely regarded as a mandatory skill for a successful Project Manager to have (Livesey, 2017).

Assuming the emotion is a response to what might be considered 'reasonable' stimulus, the Project Manager should take action to create a plan to tackle the issue. It would be prudent at this stage to inform the customer of the intention to create an action plan. However, it may also be wise to keep details reserved until all of the options are understood and certain, otherwise the customer may become more emotional.

Once the best plan is known, it should be relayed to the customer. This also requires some emotional intelligence, as the best plan may still be a trigger to the customer. However, at the stage the plan should be known as the best one, and so should definitely be shared. Purposefully holding information back may likely lead to a worsened relationship in the future. It is another immportant skill for Project Managers (in fact all managers) to be able to effectively deliver bad news (Bies, 2013).

Finally, the Project Manager must manage the plan to ensure delivery is executed as agreed with the customer. Although this can be, to some extent, out of their control, they must be focused on delivery and also communicative with the customer, providing regular updates and mitigations to challenges as they come up. This stage might be considered the more 'traditional' idea of what a Project Manager does, and should not be forgotten about when compared to newer definitions of a Project Manager.

References

Bies, R.J. (2013) The delivery of bad news in organizations: A framework for analysis. Journal of Management 39(1): 136-162.

Lindgren, M., Packendorff, J. & Sergi, V. (2014) Thrilled by the discourse, suffering through the experience: Emotions in project-based work. Human relations 67(11): 1383-1412. DOI: 10.1177/0018726713520022

Livesey, P.V. (2017). Goleman-Boyatzis model of emotional intelligence for dealing with problems in project management. Construction Economics and Building 17(1): 20-45.
